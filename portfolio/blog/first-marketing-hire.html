Building Marketing as the First Hire at 3 Startups

What I wish someone had told me about establishing marketing from scratch at early-stage companies.

10 min read January 2026

Being the first marketing hire at a startup is equal parts terrifying and exhilarating. You have unlimited potential to make an impact—and unlimited potential to fail spectacularly. I've done this three times now: at cliexa, PatientIQ, and effectively as a consultant at Ambience Healthcare. Here's what I've learned.

The Reality Check

When I joined cliexa as their first marketing hire, I thought I knew what I was getting into. I had no idea.

Here's what "first marketing hire" actually means:

  • You're not just doing marketing—you're building the entire marketing function
  • There's no playbook, no templates, no "how we've always done it"
  • Every decision creates precedent for years to come
  • You're simultaneously strategist, executor, and analyst
  • Your CEO has strong opinions about marketing but no time to do it
  • Sales is either your best friend or your biggest skeptic (often both)

By the time I joined PatientIQ a few years later, I had learned enough to know what I didn't know. And by Ambience Healthcare, I had a framework.

Lesson 1: Build Infrastructure Before Campaigns

At cliexa, I made the classic mistake: I started running campaigns before building proper infrastructure. We generated leads that went into a spreadsheet. Literally—a Google Sheet.

The result? We had no idea which campaigns actually worked. Attribution was guesswork. Lead follow-up was inconsistent. We were flying blind.

At PatientIQ, I did the opposite. Before running a single campaign, I:

My First 30 Days Framework
  1. Week 1: Audit existing tools, data, and processes (even if minimal)
  2. Week 2: Select and implement CRM (HubSpot in my case)
  3. Week 3: Set up analytics, tracking, and attribution
  4. Week 4: Create baseline reporting dashboards

Only after this foundation was in place did I start running campaigns. The difference was night and day—within 6 months, I could tell the executive team exactly which channels were driving pipeline and at what cost.

Lesson 2: Partner with Sales on Day One

At an early-stage startup, marketing and sales are the same team. Or at least, they should be.

At PatientIQ, I sat in on every sales call for the first month. Not because I needed to learn the product (though I did), but because I needed to understand:

  • What objections prospects raised
  • What language resonated vs. fell flat
  • How long deals actually took to close
  • What made a "good" lead vs. a time-waster

This investment paid off enormously. Our lead scoring model was based on real sales feedback, not marketing assumptions. Our content addressed actual objections. Our MQL definition actually meant something.

Pro tip: Schedule a weekly 30-minute sync with your top sales rep. Review leads, get feedback, adjust targeting. This single meeting will improve your marketing 10x more than any campaign optimization.

Lesson 3: The 70/20/10 Resource Split

As the only marketer, you can't do everything. I learned to allocate my time deliberately:

70%
Demand Generation

Activities that directly generate pipeline. Campaigns, content, lead nurturing.

20%
Brand & Content

Building credibility and thought leadership. Case studies, website, positioning.

10%
Experimentation

Testing new channels and tactics. Some will fail; a few will become your next big wins.

The 10% experimentation budget is crucial. At cliexa, this is how we discovered that conference-based ABM outperformed everything else. At PatientIQ, it's how we found that co-marketing with health system customers drove more qualified leads than paid advertising.

Lesson 4: Document Everything

When you're the only marketer, you're also the only one who knows how things work. If you get hit by a bus (or, more likely, take a vacation), everything stops.

I learned to document obsessively:

  • Process docs: How to run campaigns, launch emails, update the website
  • Tool access: Every login, credential, and admin contact
  • Vendor contacts: Who to call when things break
  • Campaign playbooks: Step-by-step guides for repeatable campaigns
  • Performance benchmarks: What "good" looks like for each channel

This documentation served three purposes: it let me take actual vacations, it made training future hires 10x easier, and it forced me to systematize my work instead of ad-hoc-ing everything.

Lesson 5: Quick Wins Build Credibility

When you're new, nobody trusts you yet. You need quick wins to build political capital for bigger initiatives.

My favorite quick wins:

  • Fix the website's obvious problems (broken forms, outdated content, mobile issues). Everyone notices, and it's usually quick work.
  • Set up automated lead notifications so sales knows immediately when a hot prospect comes in. Sales loves this.
  • Create one really good case study. Sales will use it in every deal. Suddenly you're "that marketer who actually helps."
  • Build a simple dashboard showing pipeline contribution. Nothing builds credibility like proving your impact with numbers.

At PatientIQ, my first quick win was setting up lead routing so sales reps got notified within 5 minutes of a demo request (vs. the next morning). Tiny change, massive impact on conversion. The sales team was suddenly very interested in what else marketing could do.

Lesson 6: Know When to Specialize

As a solo marketer, you have to be a generalist. But you can't be equally good at everything. Pick 2-3 areas to go deep on, and find workarounds for the rest.

My specializations became:

  • Marketing technology/CRM: This became my superpower. Solid infrastructure made everything else easier.
  • Demand generation: Directly tied to revenue, which is what matters at a startup.
  • Content strategy: Good content compounds over time; bad content is a constant tax.

For everything else—design, video, event logistics—I learned to use freelancers, templates, and good-enough solutions. You can't be an expert at everything, and trying will burn you out.

Lesson 7: The Metrics That Matter

Early-stage startups don't need complicated marketing metrics. Focus on these:

Pipeline Contribution

What percentage of new pipeline came from marketing sources? At PatientIQ, we hit 44%—that number got attention.

Cost Per Opportunity

How much do you spend to create a qualified opportunity? This tells you which channels to double down on.

Sales Cycle Length

Are marketing-sourced deals closing faster or slower than other sources? Faster means your targeting is good.

MQL to SQL Conversion

What percentage of marketing qualified leads become sales qualified? If it's below 30%, your MQL definition needs work.

Skip vanity metrics like social followers, website visitors, or email open rates. They feel good but don't tie to revenue.

The Honest Assessment

Being the first marketing hire isn't for everyone. It requires:

  • Comfort with ambiguity: There's no manager telling you what to do
  • Entrepreneurial mindset: You're building a business function, not filling a role
  • Thick skin: Not every idea will work, and you'll own the failures
  • Patience with chaos: Startups pivot, priorities change, budgets fluctuate
  • Self-motivation: Nobody will push you except yourself

But if this sounds exciting rather than terrifying, it's one of the most rewarding roles in marketing. You get to build something from nothing. You see direct impact on company growth. And you learn more in one year than you would in five at a large company.

After three times as the first marketing hire, I can confidently say: the playbook exists, but you have to write it yourself. These lessons are my attempt to give future first hires a head start.
